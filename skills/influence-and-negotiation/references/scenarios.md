# Worked scenarios

Seven realistic end-to-end scenarios applying the skill across its supported domains: four B2B (sales pricing pushback, enterprise RFP, services SOW, asymmetric power) plus three non-commercial (salary ask, NAO opening, clinical refusal of care). Names and numbers are illustrative; the patterns generalise.

## Scenario 1 — B2B SaaS price pushback + multi-threading

**Context.** You're an AE selling a $180k ACV observability platform to a 1500-person fintech. You've had 3 calls. The technical evaluation is going well. The CIO (your assumed Economic Buyer) just emailed: _"Love the product. Can we get to $120k? We're 30% over budget for tooling this year."_

**The trap (default LLM advice).** Offer a discount. Maybe $150k. Hope they say yes. Lose ARR.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 2 — Map the deal (revisit).** The CIO is your assumed Economic Buyer, but for $180k ACV in fintech, the actual signer is often the CFO — and the CIO has consumed your single thread for 3 calls. Diagnostic: ask your champion (the platform engineering lead) _"who else needs to weigh in on this?"_ Likely answer: CFO, sometimes the InfoSec lead, sometimes a procurement consultant retained for vendor negotiations.

**Phase 6 — Live response.** Don't reply to the email's number. Reply with calibrated questions:

> _"Thanks — really glad it's resonating. Two questions before I go price-shopping internally: (1) how was the $120k figure set — was that a top-down budget or a bottom-up model from your team? (2) when we get this through, who else needs to be comfortable with the number besides you?"_

The first question diagnoses the fixed-budget claim. The second multi-threads.

**Then trade, don't discount.** If it turns out the $120k is a real top-down budget from the CFO, you're now negotiating with the actual Economic Buyer (the CFO's budget directive) and you should ask to bring in the CFO directly:

> _"To get this through cleanly, can we set up 30 minutes with you, your CFO, and our VP of Sales? She has more flexibility than I do, and the conversation will be faster with the budget owner in the room."_

This shifts the conversation from your AE-level authority (limited) to your VP's authority (broader) AND brings the buyer's actual decision-maker into the room. Now you can trade — multi-year for the lower year-1 number, reference rights for a discount, or a structured payment schedule that fits their fiscal timing.

**Likely outcome.** Better-than-default-discount close at $160k year 1, $180k year 2 onwards, multi-year, with a public case study and a logo on the website. The trade made the smaller year-1 number palatable internally.

**The patterns illustrated.** Don't react to the price ask. Diagnose the budget claim. Multi-thread when single-threaded. Trade, don't give. Pull in air cover (your VP) when the conversation outgrows your authority.

## Scenario 2 — Enterprise procurement RFP + fiscal-year leverage

**Context.** You're an AE on a $2M TCV deal with a 50,000-person enterprise. You're 1 of 3 vendors short-listed after a 4-month RFP. Procurement has just sent the "best and final" letter on the buyer's MSA template, with redlines that include uncapped liability, no-cause termination, and a 90-day exit clause that's effectively a free pilot. They want a response in 5 business days. Your CFO is breathing down your neck for a quarter-end close.

**The trap.** Two parallel traps. (1) Sign the redlines because the deal is "too important to lose," eating uncapped liability your legal team would never approve in normal circumstances. (2) Use quarter-end internal pressure as your reason to discount, signalling to procurement that you'll fold under deadline pressure.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 1 — Mandate revisit.** Get on the phone with your VP of Sales, your General Counsel, and your CFO. Get a written mandate:

- Pricing — Mandascan with 5 points (entry $2.4M / ideal $2.2M / objective $2.0M / escalation $1.8M / rupture $1.6M).
- Legal — pre-approved redlines you can sign without re-routing. Anything else escalates.
- Term and rate — what trades does the company actually want (multi-year, payment terms, reference rights, expansion commitments)?

Without this, you're improvising under fiscal-year pressure — exactly the conditions that produce the closing pathologies (Monsieur Plus, target fascination, refus de l'échec).

**Phase 6 — Live response to procurement.** Don't engage with the redlines individually. Engage with the **structure**.

> _"Thanks for the consolidated package. To respond fully and quickly, I need to understand the structure. Can we get on a call this week with our GC and yours, plus you and me? The redlines on liability and termination are unusual at this scale, and I want to understand what's driving them — there may be a way to address the underlying concern without crossing what we can sign."_

This does three things: (1) names that the redlines are unusual, (2) brings legal into the conversation early instead of as a final-week blocker, (3) signals to procurement that you're not folding.

**Phase 4 — Plan the moves on quarter-end.** Trade your urgency.

> _"My CFO wants this done by Friday. To make that work, I can offer quarter-end pricing on a 3-year term with reference rights and a public case study. If we move past Friday, the math resets to standard rate cards. Which structure works for you?"_

You've named your urgency (so they can't hold it over you) and bound your quarter-end concession to specific trades that benefit your company in the long run.

**On the fake competing bid.** Procurement may say "we have offers from Vendor X at $1.5M." Diagnose:

> _"Help me understand what Vendor X is offering for that number — is it the same scope (data volumes, integrations, support tier)? Sometimes there's a configuration difference that explains the price gap."_

Real competing bids generate specific differentiator answers; fake ones generate vague claims. Plus, procurement may not be able to share specifics legally — which itself is signal.

**Likely outcome.** Close at $1.95M, 3-year term, reference rights, with the most aggressive redlines softened (capped liability, 60-day cure period instead of no-cause termination). quarter-end hit. Customer happy because they got quarter-end pricing for their fiscal cycle.

**The patterns illustrated.** Mandate before improvising. Don't engage redlines individually — engage the structure. Trade quarter-end urgency, don't fold to it. Diagnose fake competing bids with calibrated questions.

## Scenario 3 — Services / consulting SOW + change request

**Context.** You're a partner at a consulting firm. A client signed a $400k 6-month SOW for a transformation engagement. Three months in, the client emails: _"We need to add a new workstream — integrating with their newly acquired subsidiary. Can your team handle that within the existing budget?"_

**The trap.** Default to "yes, of course we can handle it" because the relationship matters and you don't want to seem rigid. The result: scope creep, team burnout, margin compression, and a precedent that erodes every future SOW.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 6 — Triage.** This is a tactical-type ask, not an emotional or belief-based one. The client is testing what you'll absorb. They almost certainly know the new workstream is out of scope; they're hoping you'll fold.

**The bad-faith protocol applies softly here** — there's no overt lie, but the implicit framing ("can your team handle that within the existing budget") is misleading. Don't engage with the false framing. Drop your ego, present the reality, open a face-saving exit:

> _"Glad you're thinking about us for the subsidiary integration — that's a real signal of the work going well. Quick reality check: when we scoped this engagement in February, the subsidiary acquisition wasn't on the table, so the team and timeline are sized for the original four workstreams. Adding the integration is real work — roughly [estimate] of effort._
>
> _Here are three structures that could work:_
>
> _(1) Add a change order for the integration workstream — additional $X over Y weeks, runs in parallel with the existing scope._
>
> _(2) Reprioritise: pull one of the original workstreams to a Phase 2, swap in the integration._
>
> _(3) Extend the existing engagement by [time] and absorb the integration into the team's bandwidth at a discounted rate given the existing relationship._
>
> _Want to set up 30 minutes to figure out which structure fits best?"_

The face-saving exit: _"the subsidiary wasn't on the table when we scoped this"_ — the client doesn't have to admit they were trying to slip work in. They get a clean way to either pay for the new scope or reprioritise.

**Phase 3 — Mandate (the consulting twist).** For services, the Mandascan applies to **scope** as much as to price. Per workstream, define what you'll absorb (entry / ideal), what you'll trade for (objective / escalation), and what you'll never absorb without a change order (rupture).

**Likely outcome.** Client picks option 1 (additional change order at $120k) or option 2 (reprioritise). Both protect your margin and reset the precedent for the rest of the engagement. The relationship survives because you offered three structures rather than refusing or absorbing.

**The patterns illustrated.** Don't fold to scope creep. Use the bad-faith protocol softly — don't argue the false framing, just present the reality. Offer multiple structures so the client picks (gives them agency, preserves their face). Mandascan applies to scope, not just price.

## Scenario 4 — Asymmetric power: small vendor vs. much-larger buyer

**Context.** You're a 25-person SaaS company. A Fortune 500 buyer has just made a $1.5M ACV proposal contingent on: (a) you commit to 99.99% SLA, (b) you give them MFN pricing for 5 years, (c) you grant them source-code escrow, (d) they get to lock you out of their 3 largest competitors for 24 months. Their procurement officer makes clear: _"These are standard for vendors of your size doing business with us."_

**The trap.** Sign because the deal is transformational ($1.5M ACV is 30% of your current revenue). Spend the next 18 months unable to grow because the asymmetric concessions you signed have hollowed out your unit economics, your competitive moves, and your team's bandwidth.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 1 — Mandate revisit.** Bring in your CEO, your investors, your CFO. Get clarity on what each of these terms costs you:

- 99.99% SLA → what's the engineering cost to actually deliver, and what's the credit cost when you fail?
- MFN pricing 5 years → what's the modelled revenue cost as you grow market price?
- Source-code escrow → standard, low cost, almost always grantable.
- Competitor lock-out 24 months → what's the cost of not being able to sell to their 3 largest competitors? Often catastrophic.

Without this analysis, you don't know which concessions are real costs and which are theatre.

**Phase 4 — Plan the moves.** Asymmetric power doesn't mean asymmetric concessions. The buyer's claim that _"these are standard for vendors of your size"_ is almost always tactical, not factual. Test it:

> _"Thanks for sharing the framework. Quick clarification on a few of these — the MFN clause and the competitor lock-out are unusual at our size. Can you walk me through how those terms came up? In particular: when you partner with vendors of similar scale to us, how often do those specific terms make it into the final contract?"_

The calibrated question forces them to either show real precedents (which would be surprising) or back off the "standard" framing.

**Phase 6 — Influence the four powers.** You don't have institutional power (you're 25 people, they're 100,000). You don't have situational power on procurement (their lawyers do this every day). You **do** have situational power on the product (you're the experts) and you **must** lean on relational power (the human champion-buyer relationship that bypasses procurement).

Multi-thread aggressively. Find the actual technical buyer who needs your product to ship a feature their CEO is asking about. Make them aware that the procurement terms are forcing you to walk away (they don't want that).

**The walk-away lever.** Asymmetric power is asymmetric only if you cannot walk away. Once you can — once your mandate explicitly authorises _"no deal"_ on these terms — the dynamic flips. Procurement is not used to small vendors walking away with discipline. The principle from Phase 0 applies: the party more emotionally invested loses, including in asymmetric situations.

> _"I appreciate the offer. We've worked through it carefully internally, and three of the four terms — SLA, MFN, and competitor lock-out — would constrain our business in ways we can't accept at this scale. We're happy to grant source-code escrow, and we'd love to find a structure that works. But if the four-term package is non-negotiable, we'd rather pass on this engagement and revisit when our relative scale changes. What would you like to do?"_

**Likely outcomes:**

- The buyer drops the lock-out and the MFN entirely, keeps the SLA at 99.9% (achievable for you), and you sign source-code escrow. Deal closes at terms you can sustainably deliver on.
- Or: the buyer is genuinely unwilling to flex and you pass. The 18 months of capacity you'd have spent on this contract goes into the next 5 deals. Net better outcome.

**The patterns illustrated.** Asymmetric power is not asymmetric concessions. The walk-away lever flips the dynamic. Test "standard" claims with calibrated questions. Multi-thread to bypass procurement when procurement is the only obstacle. Lean on the powers you actually have (situational on product, relational with the human buyer) when you don't have institutional or scale power.

## Scenario 5 — Salary ask facing "envelope closed" + counter-offer

**Context.** You're a senior staff engineer at a 600-person growth-stage SaaS company. You've been at $185k base for two years; market for your role is now $215k–$240k. You've taken on tech-lead scope on top of IC work. You raised the ask 3 weeks ago. Your manager just replied: _"I really value your impact. The envelope's closed for this cycle — let's revisit at the next review in 8 months."_ Two days later, a recruiter from a peer company makes a verbal offer at $235k base + $80k sign-on + meaningful equity refresh.

**The trap.** Two parallel traps. (1) Accept the "next review" deferral and let 8 months pass without a structural commitment. (2) Use the external offer as a threat ("match this or I leave"), forcing your manager into reactive matching mode that may resolve the compensation gap but breaks the trust contract — which is half the reason most counter-offer-accepters leave within 12 months anyway.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 3 — Mandascan multi-axis.** Don't reduce this to base. Five axes:

- Base salary
- Equity refresh (size + vesting cliff + acceleration trigger)
- Title / scope (tech-lead recognised, not just absorbed)
- One-off retention bonus (separates from base envelope question)
- Review cadence (cycle-aligned commitment, not vague "next review")

For each, sketch entry / ideal / objective / escalation / rupture (see [references/preparation.md](preparation.md#the-mandascan--5-points-per-axis)).

**Phase 6 — Live response to "envelope closed."** Don't argue the envelope claim and don't fold to it. Use the reality-principle script:

> _"I hear the envelope's closed for the standard cycle, and I appreciate the directness. Help me understand the constraint better. Is it base envelope specifically, total compensation, or department-level? And if base is fixed for this cycle, what would unlock movement — a structural exception (sign-on, equity refresh tied to the tech-lead scope), or a cycle-aligned commitment with a specific number now and a date?"_

This forces the manager to either name the actual constraint (often base specifically — equity, sign-on, and one-off retention are different envelopes) or to escalate.

**On the external offer — the trust frame.** The natural move is to use the offer as leverage. The disciplined move is to share it as **information**, not as a threat:

> _"For context, I'm not actively interviewing, but a recruiter at [adjacent company] made an unprompted offer last week — $235k base, sign-on, equity refresh. I'm sharing because I want you to have full context, not because I'm using it as leverage. I'd rather solve this with you than walk. Help me understand what's possible — and if the answer is genuinely 'nothing structural this cycle,' I'd rather know that now than in 8 months."_

This does three things: (1) puts a real number in the room without a hostage-style threat, (2) explicitly disclaims the leverage move (which procurement-trained managers respect), (3) introduces a firm timeline ("now, not in 8 months") that converts the deferral into a forced choice.

**The escalation move.** If the immediate manager can't or won't move, request the structural conversation: _"Who has the authority on a structural exception? I'd like to make the case once, in front of whoever can approve it, rather than do this in pieces."_ Forces a real conversation rather than a series of soft "I checked, can't do it" replies.

**Likely outcomes.**

- **Best:** company comes back with a sign-on bonus + equity refresh + tech-lead title + commitment to a base raise at the next cycle (specific number, specific date). You stay. Trust intact.
- **Acceptable:** company can't structurally match but offers a real package (e.g., equity refresh + title) that closes 60% of the gap. You decide whether to accept or take the external offer. Either way, decision is informed.
- **Worst case:** company takes the deferral position even after the structured ask. You take the external offer. The conversation taught you something about how this company values retention; that information is useful for your career planning regardless.

**On accepting a counter-offer.** Decision rule: take the counter-offer ONLY if the proposal includes a structural change (new role, new scope, new manager, written cycle-aligned commitment). If it's just a base match, decline — the trust contract has shifted, and statistically half of base-only counter-offer-acceptors leave within 12 months anyway. The trust frame: _"They needed me to threaten to leave before paying me fairly."_

**The patterns illustrated.** Multi-axis Mandascan, not just base. Don't argue the envelope, diagnose it. Share external offers as information, not threat. Force structural exceptions over vague deferrals. Rule for accepting counter-offers: structural change required.

## Scenario 6 — NAO opening + strike de-escalation

**Context.** You're the DRH of a 1,200-person industrial site. NAO opens in two weeks. Sector wage growth has been 2.8% YoY; your company's net margin dropped from 4.1% to 2.6% over the last 18 months due to raw-material cost inflation. The CGT delegation is asking for 5% across-the-board plus a 13th month bonus and a structural reduction in overtime. The previous DRH gave the team _"on ne lâche rien"_ before NAO last year, and an 8-day strike followed.

**The trap.** Two parallel traps. (1) Repeat the previous DRH's error — give a fuzzy mandate ("hold the line, see what happens") and improvise live, which produces concession errors, escalation, or another strike. (2) Pre-concede on the headline number to avoid a strike, signalling weakness that union delegates will exploit on every secondary axis (RTT, telework, training budget).

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 1 — Mandate co-construction with the décideur (CEO + CFO).** Get a written mandate, axis-by-axis, signed by COMEX:

- Augmentation générale — Mandascan with 5 points (entry 0.5% / ideal 1.2% / objective 1.8% / bascule 2.4% (call CFO) / rupture 3.0% (no agreement)).
- Augmentation individuelle — separate envelope; what % of payroll for merit allocation.
- Primes (one-off, intéressement, profit-sharing) — easier to grant because they don't compound, harder to demand year-over-year.
- Working time / RTT / telework — non-monetary, often higher psychological value to delegates than base raise (and to membership in some cohorts).
- Training budget — commitment, not money out the door this year.

Without this written mandate, the team has no margin to trade — the exact condition that produced last year's 8-day strike. The 5-point Mandascan also gives the team **room to make and trade concessions**, which is what the union delegates need to be able to bring an agreement back to membership.

**Phase 4 — OCP statement, written, in opening session.** Open with the shared objective in plain language:

> _"Before we open the specific demands, I want to share the frame I'm working in. Our shared objective: keep this site operating sustainably so we guarantee the 1,200 jobs here over the next three years. Sector wage growth has been 2.8%; our net margin has dropped from 4.1% to 2.6%. The number we land on has to fit inside both of those constraints. I'm here to negotiate hard within that frame, not outside it. Are you willing to work within that frame too?"_

If the delegates accept the OCP, you've shifted from positionnisme to coopétition. If they refuse it openly ("you're trying to box us in"), you've learned the strategy is compétition this round — adjust accordingly (point-par-point, no globalisation, no early concession).

**Phase 6 — Live response to aggressive moves.**

- **Punching-ball.** A delegate raises voice, calls the proposal "an insult to workers." Verbalise the legitimate emotion, refuse the unacceptable behaviour: _"I can hear the frustration — the gap between 5% and what's possible is real, and I want to address it. The tone won't help us reach an agreement. Let's take 20 minutes and come back."_ Pause tactique. If the behaviour resumes, end the session and reschedule.
- **Ultimatum.** _"Without 4% confirmed by Friday, we file the strike notice."_ Diagnose: _"What specifically happens at Friday? Is that a CSE meeting trigger, a federation directive, or a tactical deadline?"_ Real deadlines have specific consequences; tactical ones evaporate under the question.
- **Coopétition simulée.** A delegate publicly accepts the OCP and then privately uses an internal-election deadline to push the lead delegate toward a hard line. Track the gap between public position and private moves; surface it without escalation: _"It seems there's pressure inside the delegation that's harder than the public position. Can we talk about what's actually constraining the lead delegate, separately from the round?"_

**Phase 6 — When a strike is already in progress.** Strikes and negotiation are positively correlated, not negatively — research consistently shows the most active negotiators face strikes most often, because strikes are part of the bargaining sequence, not a failure of dialogue. Don't treat the strike as catastrophe; treat it as a costly but predictable signal:

- Don't concede mid-strike under stress — that teaches the delegation the move works and trains the next one.
- Identify the **single non-negotiable issue** for the union (often face-saving, sometimes a structural item that's smaller than it sounds). Resolving that one item, even with a symbolic concession, often unblocks the whole round.
- Use the strike days to deepen sociogramme — which delegates are pushing for resolution, which are extending? Multi-thread to the resolution-pushers.

**Likely outcomes.**

- **Cooperation:** OCP accepted; agreement reached after 2–3 sessions at ~1.8% AGS + restructured primes + telework expansion + training-budget commitment. No strike.
- **Coopétition:** OCP partially accepted; brief 2-day strike as the delegation needs to bring something back to membership; agreement at ~2.0% with structural improvements that are harder to walk back at next year's NAO.
- **Compétition:** OCP refused, hard strike (5+ days), agreement only after federation involvement. Outcome similar but trust damaged for next year.

**The patterns illustrated.** Co-built written mandate, not "on ne lâche rien". OCP in writing in opening session. Don't concede mid-strike. Identify the union's single non-negotiable. Sociogramme of delegates by their internal incentives (election cycles, federation pressure, membership demographics).

## Scenario 7 — Clinical refusal of care

**Context.** You're a hospital oncologist. Your patient — a 42-year-old mother of two with a stage III breast cancer diagnosis — refuses adjuvant chemotherapy after surgery. The medical case for treatment is strong (estimated 5-year survival improvement +18 percentage points). Her stated reason: _"I can't be sick again for my kids — I'd rather take the risk."_ Her husband and oncology nurse are urging her to reconsider.

**The trap.** Three parallel traps. (1) Argue the medical merits — the survival numbers, the protocol details — which lands as logical refutation against an emotional position and produces dug-in refusal. (2) Defer to autonomy without surfacing the actual enjeu — accepting the surface "no" without the deeper conversation that might change it. (3) Recruit the family to apply pressure — which creates a coercion frame, breaks the alliance, and often produces compliance without adherence (she takes the first cycle, then "forgets" the second).

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 1 — Setting + perception (SPIKES P-step).** Schedule a private conversation with the patient alone first (family separately afterwards if she chooses). Phones off. Both seated. Open with perception, not information:

> _"Before I say anything more, I want to understand where you are. What's going through your mind about the diagnosis, and what's making chemotherapy feel like the wrong choice right now?"_

This invitation surfaces the actual frame she's in. Her words tell you whether the refusal is rooted in fear of the side effects (suffering), fear of the impact on her children (mother-identity), distrust of medicine, religious belief, or something else — and the response strategy is completely different for each.

**Phase 3 — Mandascan reframed for clinical.** The substance is the medically defensible care plan. The form is everything else — timing, sequencing, support structure, what the kids see, what the workplace knows. The mandate is to find a plan she'll actually adhere to over 12+ months — perfect protocol with 0% adherence is worse than partial protocol with 90% adherence.

| Axis | Entry | Ideal | Objective | Escalation | Rupture |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Treatment regimen | Full standard protocol | 90% adherence (one schedule modification) | 80% adherence (modified schedule + symptom management plan) | <70% (escalate to consultant for re-assessment) | Refusal of all curative treatment (palliative pathway) |

**Phase 6 — Surface the enjeu (POE).** Her position is _"no chemo."_ Her objectif is _"protect my kids from seeing me sick."_ The likely enjeu is mother-identity: she experiences treatment as becoming the sick mother her own family member was, or as exposing her kids to fear, or as becoming someone she doesn't recognise. Surface it:

> _"What you said earlier — about not being sick again for your kids — I want to make sure I understand. Is the fear about the side effects themselves, about what your kids will see, about how you'll feel about yourself, or something else I'm not seeing?"_

The answer reshapes the conversation entirely. If it's "what my kids will see," the conversation is about scheduling, support structure, and what childcare looks like during low-energy weeks — not about cancer biology. If it's "how I'll feel about myself," the conversation involves an oncology psychologist before treatment starts.

**Phase 6 — NURSE statements before any treatment recommendation.**

- **N**ame: _"This is one of the hardest decisions someone can face."_
- **U**nderstand: _"It makes complete sense to weigh treatment against what your kids will live through."_
- **R**espect: _"You've been thinking about this carefully — that's the right way to approach it."_
- **S**upport: _"Whatever you decide, I'm not going anywhere — we'll work through this together."_
- **E**xplore: _"Tell me what a 'good outcome' would look like for you a year from now, given everything you're weighing."_

**Phase 6 — Form vs substance handoff.** Once the enjeu is surfaced, hand control of the form, preserve the substance:

> _"On the medicine, the recommendation is clear — chemo materially improves your outcome. That's my job to be honest about. On how — when we start, what schedule fits your kids' calendar, who supports you on hard weeks, what we tell your employer, whether your psychologist sees you alongside this — that's all yours to shape. I want a plan you'll actually live for the next twelve months, not the textbook plan that you'll abandon after cycle two."_

This separates the medical fact (chemo recommended) from the operational form (schedule, support, framing). She gets agency over the form and is far more likely to adhere.

**Phase 6 — Bad-news family conversation.** Separately, with her permission, address the family:

- The husband and nurse pushing for compliance can become saboteurs of the alliance if they're not coached. Brief them: _"What's going to determine whether she completes treatment isn't pressure — it's her sense of control. I need you to back off the 'you have to do this' frame and become her support team. I'll handle the medicine; you handle the kids and the daily life."_
- Reframe their role from "convince her" to "support whatever she decides, with my honest medical recommendation as a third voice in the room."

**Phase 7 — Follow-up cadence.** First cycle is the riskiest moment for non-adherence. Schedule the follow-up call yourself, not via the nurse, 48–72 hours after cycle 1. Don't ask "did you take it" — ask _"how did the first 48 hours go, and what's coming up that worries you about the next two weeks?"_ Pre-emptive support has higher adherence yield than reactive correction.

**Likely outcomes.**

- **Best:** patient agrees to a modified-schedule protocol (e.g., dose-dense schedule that compresses treatment, or split-week dosing that protects weekend energy for the kids). She completes treatment. Adherence high because the plan was co-designed.
- **Acceptable:** patient agrees to the standard protocol after surfacing the enjeu and getting the form-control language; outcome similar to best case.
- **Pathway change:** the actual enjeu turns out to be a conviction the medical case is weaker than presented, or a belief in alternative treatment, or a faith-based stance — and the conversation shifts toward shared decision-making with the alternatives genuinely on the table. Sometimes the right outcome is for the patient to refuse curative treatment, fully informed; the negotiator's job is to make sure the refusal is informed and not driven by an unaddressed enjeu.
- **Worst case:** family pressure overrides the alliance, patient accepts treatment under duress, drops out after cycle 1. This is the failure mode the playbook is designed to prevent.

**The patterns illustrated.** Perception-before-knowledge (SPIKES P-step). Surface the enjeu before arguing the substance. Form-vs-substance handoff. NURSE statements before any recommendation. Coach the family (don't recruit them as enforcers). Pre-emptive follow-up after cycle 1.
