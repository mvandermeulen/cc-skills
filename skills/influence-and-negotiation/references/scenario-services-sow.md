# Scenario: Services / consulting SOW + change request

**Context.** You're a partner at a consulting firm. A client signed a $400k 6-month SOW for a transformation engagement. Three months in, the client emails: _"We need to add a new workstream — integrating with their newly acquired subsidiary. Can your team handle that within the existing budget?"_

**The trap.** Default to "yes, of course we can handle it" because the relationship matters and you don't want to seem rigid. The result: scope creep, team burnout, margin compression, and a precedent that erodes every future SOW.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 6 — Triage.** This is a tactical-type ask, not an emotional or belief-based one. The client is testing what you'll absorb. They almost certainly know the new workstream is out of scope; they're hoping you'll fold.

**The bad-faith protocol applies softly here** — there's no overt lie, but the implicit framing ("can your team handle that within the existing budget") is misleading. Don't engage with the false framing. Drop your ego, present the reality principle, open a face-saving exit:

> _"Glad you're thinking about us for the subsidiary integration — that's a real signal of the work going well. Quick reality check: when we scoped this engagement in February, the subsidiary acquisition wasn't on the table, so the team and timeline are sized for the original four workstreams. Adding the integration is real work — roughly [estimate] of effort._
>
> _Here are three structures that could work:_
>
> _(1) Add a change order for the integration workstream — additional $X over Y weeks, runs in parallel with the existing scope._
>
> _(2) Reprioritise: pull one of the original workstreams to a Phase 2, swap in the integration._
>
> _(3) Extend the existing engagement by [time] and absorb the integration into the team's bandwidth at a discounted rate given the existing relationship._
>
> _Want to set up 30 minutes to figure out which structure fits best?"_

The face-saving exit: _"the subsidiary wasn't on the table when we scoped this"_ — the client doesn't have to admit they were trying to slip work in. They get a clean way to either pay for the new scope or reprioritise.

**Phase 3 — Mandate (the consulting twist).** For services, the Mandascan applies to **scope** as much as to price. Per workstream, define what you'll absorb (entry / ideal), what you'll trade for (objective / escalation), and what you'll never absorb without a change order (rupture).

**Likely outcome.** Client picks option 1 (additional change order at $120k) or option 2 (reprioritise). Both protect your margin and reset the precedent for the rest of the engagement. The relationship survives because you offered three structures rather than refusing or absorbing.

**The patterns illustrated.** Don't fold to scope creep. Use the bad-faith protocol softly — don't argue the false framing, just present the reality. Offer multiple structures so the client picks (gives them agency, preserves their face). Mandascan applies to scope, not just price.
