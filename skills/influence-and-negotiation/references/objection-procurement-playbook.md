# Procurement playbook awareness

What enterprise procurement teams are trained to do. Not paranoia — just awareness. Sellers who don't know these patterns walk into them blind.

### The escalation ladder

Classic three-bite play:

1. Primary contact extracts concession #1.
2. _"Procurement won't approve this; we need another N%."_
3. _"CFO says we're outside acceptable range; final ask is X."_

**Counter.** Name it: _"We've discussed pricing twice already. I'd love to understand who has the final authority so we can have one conversation rather than three."_ Anchor each concession to a trade. Refuse incremental discounts without reopening scope.

### The fixed-budget claim

_"Our budget is $X, that's all we have."_ Treated as objective constraint by sellers. Reality: budgets are flexible, staged, often understated.

**Counter.** _"How was that number set?"_ / _"What would unlock additional budget for the right outcome?"_ Both are calibrated, both surface that the budget is not a hard wall.

### Fake competing bid

The "stalking horse" play. Procurement keeps a real or fictitious competitor alive to maintain leverage.

**Counter.** Diagnose with _"What does the alternative offer that we don't?"_ and _"Help me understand what's keeping us in the conversation."_ Real competitors generate specific differentiator answers; fake ones generate platitudes. If you suspect a fake, focus on differentiation that's genuinely hard to replicate (your product, not your price).

### Free pilot / POC

Procurement frames pilots as "low-risk evaluation" but uses them to: anchor pilot pricing as the new baseline, defer commitment indefinitely, extract product knowledge without a contract.

**Counter.** Paid pilots only, scope-locked, with a **pre-agreed conversion clause** (date, price, term). Pilot pricing tied to full-contract pricing, not standalone.

### Fiscal-year leverage

Buyers research vendor fiscal calendars and time final negotiations to vendor Q4 or FY-end. Saved value: 20–40% of contract when timed and prepared.

**Counter.** Don't hide your urgency — **trade** it. _"If we close by Friday, I can offer Q4 pricing on a multi-year term with reference rights. If we slip to next quarter, the math changes."_ Your urgency is real; the discipline is to not let it become the buyer's lever.

### MSA redline tactics

Buyer drafts the MSA on **their paper** to anchor terms. Procurement and legal cycles drag 1–3 months. Standard target redlines: data security, uncapped breach liability, sandbox rights, affiliate use, change-control on service capabilities, exit / data portability, price-cap clauses, no auto-renewal.

**Counter.** Pre-approved redline library — common modifications your legal team has signed off on, so the negotiator can agree without a 2-week legal queue. Send your MSA first when possible.

**Late-stage redline injection.** A specific procurement variant: legal redlines arrive 24–72 hours before the planned signature, often on items that weren't raised during the negotiation. The pressure to close vs the cost of renegotiating produces over-concession.

_Counter script._ "Adding new terms at signature changes the deal structure. If we hold the original signature date, the discount we agreed to comes off the table — I need the trade to justify the new terms internally. If you'd rather keep the discount, we can extend the signature date to allow proper review." This forces a choice — date or price — and refuses the false binary the procurement move sets up.

### MFN clauses — the AE-escalation default

Most-favoured-nation clauses ("we get the best price you give any customer of similar size for the next N years") are the most under-recognised concession AEs make. They constrain every future deal, every promo, every M&A bundling.

**Default rule.** MFN clauses are NOT in the AE's signing authority. Always escalate to legal/CRO, regardless of how junior the buyer's request seems. The cost compounds across multiple future deals; what looks like a small concession in this deal becomes a multi-quarter constraint on the entire customer book.

**Counter when buyer pushes.** "MFN is one of the few clauses I can't sign — the trade-off would affect deals that don't exist yet. I can give you a different protection — a price hold for N months on this configuration if your scope grows — that achieves the underlying goal without the structural exposure."

### Nibbling — the post-agreement small ask

Once the deal is verbally closed but before paper signature, the buyer asks for a series of small extras: an additional licence for the test environment, training included, a few extra seats for an adjacent team, a custom report. Each individually small; cumulatively expensive.

Why it works: the negotiator has mentally booked the deal and treats the asks as paperwork friction.

**Counter rule.** Always trade, never gift. _"This would normally be a $X line item. If you want it, here's what works: extend term to 36 months / add a reference / multi-year payment commit." Refuse to cumulate freebies even when they feel small._

### Flinch — visible shock at price

Buyer reacts to your price with theatrical surprise — sharp inhale, _"that's a lot,"_ visible recoil. The move is designed to pressure you into apologising or pre-conceding.

**Counter script.** "What were you expecting?" Said neutrally, with no apology. This pivots the conversation from your price to **their** anchor — usually a number with no underlying basis. Often they cannot answer cleanly, which neutralises the move.

### Negative reverse — for ghosted deals

A counterintuitive but well-validated tactic for the radio-silent buyer at late stage: when the buyer has gone silent for 10–14 days after a clear ask, send the **opposite** of a chase email.

_Script._ "I haven't heard back, and I want to make sure I'm not assuming something that's not there. Should I close the opportunity for now, or is there something specific I should be solving for?"

This invites a "no" (which the counterparty experiences as safe — see [references/tactics.md](tactics.md#the-power-of-no)) and produces honest signal fast. Three outcomes:

- "Yes, close it" — saves you months of misallocated effort.
- "No, we're still working on it, here's what's happening" — re-opens the conversation with new context.
- No reply — confirms the deal is dead; move on.

### Good cop / bad cop force-alignment

Two procurement contacts — one collaborative, one hostile — extracting concessions from both sides. The "good cop" softens you for the bad cop's hard ask.

**Counter.** Name it without escalation. _"I want to make sure I'm hearing one position. I can negotiate against one set of terms — I can't negotiate against two. Which of you should I be aligning with?"_ Forces them to either consolidate or expose the dynamic. Either result removes the leverage.

### Bogey — the false-priority diversion

Buyer feigns importance on a secondary item ("the SLA tier is non-negotiable for us") to "trade" later for a real concession on the primary item ("we can compromise on the SLA if you give us 20% on price"). The "trade" looks reciprocal but isn't — the secondary item never mattered to them.

**Counter.** Map every concession against your Mandascan **before** trading. The contrepartie has to match the actual axis value, not the buyer's stated emphasis. If you suspect a bogey, test: _"Help me understand why the SLA tier is so important — what specifically would change for your team at the next tier?"_ Real importance has specifics; bogeys produce vague reassurance.

### Anchor-then-stall

Buyer drops an extreme low anchor early, then goes silent for 2–3 weeks, hoping the negotiator will come back with a desperate counter-offer rather than hold the line.

**Counter.** Don't move first. The anchor stays unanswered until the buyer re-engages. If they don't re-engage, they were testing your discipline — and concession would have set the new floor for the rest of the deal.

### The 70/30 rule

Procurement is trained to talk 30%, listen 70%. Negotiators trained to do the reverse fall into one-sided concession patterns. **Match their cadence.** Talk less. Ask more. Let them fill the silence.
