# Scenario: B2B SaaS price pushback + multi-threading

**Context.** You're an AE selling a $180k ACV observability platform to a 1500-person fintech. You've had 3 calls. The technical evaluation is going well. The CIO (your assumed Economic Buyer) just emailed: _"Love the product. Can we get to $120k? We're 30% over budget for tooling this year."_

**The trap (default LLM advice).** Offer a discount. Maybe $150k. Hope they say yes. Lose ARR.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 2 — Map the deal (revisit).** The CIO is your assumed Economic Buyer, but for $180k ACV in fintech, the actual signer is often the CFO — and the CIO has consumed your single thread for 3 calls. Diagnostic: ask your champion (the platform engineering lead) _"who else needs to weigh in on this?"_ Likely answer: CFO, sometimes the InfoSec lead, sometimes a procurement consultant retained for vendor negotiations.

**Phase 6 — Live response.** Don't reply to the email's number. Reply with calibrated questions:

> _"Thanks — really glad it's resonating. Two questions before I go price-shopping internally: (1) how was the $120k figure set — was that a top-down budget or a bottom-up model from your team? (2) when we get this through, who else needs to be comfortable with the number besides you?"_

The first question diagnoses the fixed-budget claim. The second multi-threads.

**Then trade, don't discount.** If it turns out the $120k is a real top-down budget from the CFO, you're now negotiating with the actual Economic Buyer (the CFO's budget directive) and you should ask to bring in the CFO directly:

> _"To get this through cleanly, can we set up 30 minutes with you, your CFO, and our VP of Sales? She has more flexibility than I do, and the conversation will be faster with the budget owner in the room."_

This shifts the conversation from your AE-level authority (limited) to your VP's authority (broader) AND brings the buyer's actual decision-maker into the room. Now you can trade — multi-year for the lower year-1 number, reference rights for a discount, or a structured payment schedule that fits their fiscal timing.

**Likely outcome.** Better-than-default-discount close at $160k year 1, $180k year 2 onwards, multi-year, with a public case study and a logo on the website. The trade made the smaller year-1 number palatable internally.

**The patterns illustrated.** Don't react to the price ask. Diagnose the budget claim. Multi-thread when single-threaded. Trade, don't give. Pull in air cover (your VP) when the conversation outgrows your authority.
