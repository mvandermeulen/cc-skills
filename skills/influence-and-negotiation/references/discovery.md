# Discovery

Discovery is where deals are won or lost — and the same principle applies in salary negotiation, recruitment, and difficult management conversations. This file covers qualification frameworks (MEDDIC/MEDDPICC), the discovery question framework (SPIN), the conversation contract (upfront agreement), the calibrated-question playbook, and behavioural reading.

The mainstream consensus across modern professional negotiation: layer the frameworks. MEDDPICC for qualification + SPIN for discovery questions + tactical empathy for live response. Don't pick one.

## MEDDPICC (extends MEDDIC)

A B2B sales qualification methodology developed at scale-stage enterprise software companies. The right tool for $50K+ ACV with 5+ stakeholders and 90+ day cycles. MEDDPICC adds Paper process and Competition for enterprise (>$100K ACV). Roughly 73% of SaaS companies selling above $100K ARR use it.

The letters:

- **M**etrics — quantified outcome the buyer cares about. Not "improve efficiency" — _"reduce billing errors from 4% to 0.5% saving $X per quarter."_
- **E**conomic buyer — who actually signs. Not who has the title; who has the spend authority for this deal size.
- **D**ecision criteria — the explicit checklist they'll evaluate against (RFP requirements, security review, integration depth).
- **D**ecision process — the internal buying steps from their side. Who reviews when. Who signs in what order. How long each step typically takes.
- **I**dentify pain — the business problem with consequences attached. "Pain" without consequences is just preference.
- **C**hampion — internal advocate with both willingness AND political capital to push for you behind closed doors.
- **(P)aper process** — procurement, legal, security, finance. The MSA template, the redline cycles, the InfoSec questionnaire. This is where 30–60 day delays hide.
- **(C)ompetition** — who else is in the deal, real or anchored. Asked directly: _"Who else are you evaluating?"_ and indirectly via calibrated questions.

A common mistake: AEs grade themselves on MEDDIC and pass a deal forward when they have 4 of 6 letters. The point of the framework is not to score; it's to find the missing letters and fill them. Stage gate: don't move a deal past Solution Validation until all letters are filled with **named people and specific facts**, not assumptions.

### Champion validation — the 3-question commitment test

Three calibrated questions in sequence:

1. _"How will we know we're on target?"_
2. _"What will we do if we're not?"_
3. _"How are we going to close this?"_

Real champions answer all three with specifics. Pseudo-champions deflect, generalise, or change topic. Diagnose, don't manipulate — if they fail the test, the issue is not their motivation, it's that they don't have the political capital you need. Find another champion or build this one's capital.

## SPIN Selling

A discovery framework built on tens of thousands of recorded sales calls. **Discovery framework, not qualification.** The S-P-I-N sequence:

- **S**ituation — current state. Use sparingly; buyers find these tedious. ("How many users do you have on the legacy system?") Do as much Situation preparation from public sources as possible so you spend the call on Problem and Implication.
- **P**roblem — pains and dissatisfactions. ("What's the most frustrating thing about how you do this today?")
- **I**mplication — the consequences of those problems. **This is where deals are won.** ("What happens to your billing accuracy when a controller is on vacation? What does that cost you per quarter?") You're building the cost of inaction.
- **N**eed-payoff — the buyer articulates the value of solving. ("If you could close the books in 5 days instead of 15, what would that enable?") You don't pitch — they sell themselves.

Implication is where mediocre AEs collapse. They go from Problem straight to demo, missing the consequence step that makes the deal urgent. The "happy ears" trap (cognitive bias toward positive signals) lives in skipped Implication. Test pain depth with: _"What happens if you do nothing?"_ Real pain has a specific, dated consequence; preference doesn't.

## Upfront contract

The opening move of any consultative-selling call. Establish, in the first 90 seconds, an explicit agreement on:

- The agenda (what you'll cover)
- The time (how long)
- The possible outcomes — including _"no"_ as a legitimate result.

Template: _"I want to make sure we use this 30 minutes well. My plan was to walk through your current state, share two things I think would matter, and figure out together if there's a fit. By the end, you'll know if this is worth a next step or not — and 'not' is a totally fine outcome. Does that work?"_

Why it works: it removes the buyer's defensive crouch (_"this guy is going to pitch me"_) and earns you permission to ask harder questions. Reduces wasted cycles dramatically. Common mistake: negotiators treat it as a script and rush through it; the value is in the counterparty's verbal _"yes, that works"_ — that's the contract.

The same pattern applies in domains beyond commercial selling: a hiring manager opens an interview with _"my plan is to spend 20 minutes on your background, then dig into one specific project, then leave 10 minutes for your questions — by the end you'll know whether to advance or not"_; a manager opens a difficult one-on-one with _"I want to share something hard, hear your reaction, and figure out together what we do — does that work as a structure?"_

## Calibrated questions library

**No "why."** "Why did you choose Vendor X?" lands as accusation. Replace with "what" / "how" formulations.

Forbidden: _"Why?"_ / _"Why did you do that?"_ Replacement: _"What led you there?"_ / _"What was the thinking behind that?"_

A library of 20 calibrated questions, organised by what they surface:

**Surface the enjeu (deeper stake):**

- _"What does success look like for you personally on this?"_
- _"What's at stake if this works / doesn't work?"_
- _"What would your CFO / board say about this approach?"_
- _"How does this connect to what you're being measured on this year?"_

**Surface the decision process:**

- _"Walk me through how a decision like this typically gets made here."_
- _"Who else needs to weigh in before this moves forward?"_
- _"What's happened the last time you bought something like this?"_
- _"By what date do you need this solved, and what's driving that date?"_

**Surface blockers (negative framing):**

- _"What would cause this not to happen?"_
- _"What's the strongest argument against doing this?"_
- _"Who in your organisation would push back hardest on this?"_

**Deflect unreasonable demands:**

- _"How am I supposed to do that?"_ (canonical line — works across sales, salary, NAO, internal management)
- _"What would you do in my position?"_
- _"Help me understand what makes that the right number for you."_

**Test commitment:**

- _"How will we know we're on target?"_
- _"What will we do if we're not?"_
- _"How are we going to close this?"_

**Surface budget reality:**

- _"How was that budget number set?"_
- _"What would unlock additional budget for the right outcome?"_
- _"Walk me through how budget approval works here."_

Use these instead of pitching. The buyer's answers carry the deal forward; your statements rarely do.

## Behavioural reading

Four observable signal families worth tracking, each with detect / interpret / react.

### Micro-expressions — 7 widely documented emotions

The 7 facial emotions most consistently documented in cross-cultural research (anger, fear, joy, sadness, surprise, disgust, contempt) flash for ½ to 1/25 of a second, often before the cortex suppresses them. You won't catch them all live; record calls (with permission), review afterward. Even imperfect detection rate beats no detection.

| Emotion | Detect | Interpret | React tactically |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Anger** | Brows drawn down and together (lion's wrinkle), lips pressed or rolled inward | Sense of injustice, attempt to dominate | Let them vent without interrupting; verbalise the emotion separately from the behaviour: _"I can hear the frustration. Help me understand what triggered it."_ |
| **Fear** | Brows raised and drawn together, upper eyelid raised (sclera visible above iris), lips pulled horizontally | Worry about consequences, threat to a vital stake | Secure with facts; offer an OCP framing — see [references/tactics.md](tactics.md#ocp--objectif-commun-partagé). Never argue past fear — the cortex isn't online. |
| **Joy** | Lip corners raised + orbicularis oculi contracted (crow's feet) | Genuine satisfaction with the proposal — even when the stated position remains hard | Maintain your offer. The micro-expression of joy is the strongest signal that you've hit the buyer's enjeu. Concede further and you're paying for something already won. |
| **Sadness** | Inner brow corners raised, eyelids drooping, lip corners pulled down | Sense of loss, fatigue with the conflict | Verbalise to neutralise the limbic activation; bring the discussion back to facts gently. _"It seems like this is wearing on the team — let's take stock of where we actually are."_ |
| **Surprise** | Brows arched, eyes wide (sclera visible), mouth slack-open | Unexpected information, momentary destabilisation | Watch the **next** expression. Surprise alone is neutral — it's followed within ~1 second by fear (bad news for them) or joy (good news). The follower tells you the actual valence. |
| **Disgust** | Nose wrinkled, upper lip raised | Visceral rejection of the proposal or the person | Change angle — either reframe the proposal or pause the meeting. Ploughing through disgust amplifies it. |
| **Contempt** | Asymmetric expression — one corner of the lip raised or pulled back | Felt superiority or dismissal toward you specifically | Rebuild your legitimacy via situational power (relevant expertise, named precedent, customer in their sector). Institutional power claims ("as the VP…") will deepen contempt — see [references/tactics.md](tactics.md#the-four-powers-of-negotiation). |

The diagnostic discipline: when you see one, **slow down**. Don't pivot the conversation immediately — the buyer will read that as detection and shut the channel. Continue the surface conversation while internally adjusting your next move.

### Latence — response delay

The pause between your question and their answer. Calibrate against the buyer's baseline first; some people are simply slow speakers.

- **Long latence on a simple question** — _"What's your fiscal year-end?"_ producing a 5-second pause — signals constructed answer (maybe a lie, maybe sensitive context). Use silence plein after the answer to force elaboration.
- **Short latence on a complex question** — _"Walk me through how you'd justify this to your CFO?"_ producing an immediate fluent answer — signals canned/rehearsed response. Probe for the seam: _"What's the part of that explanation you're least confident about?"_
- **Increasing latence over a session** — early answers fast, later answers slow — signals fatigue or that you're approaching a sensitive area. Either pause the conversation or gently retreat to safer ground and return later.

### Proxémie — physical distance and orientation

The four conventional proxemic zones (research consensus):

| Zone | Distance | Meaning when adopted |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Intimate | 0–45 cm | Reserved for trust + private conversations; rare in professional contexts |
| Personnelle | 45–60 cm | Comfort, confidence — the counterparty is open |
| Sociale | 60–360 cm | Neutral / formal — default for first meetings |
| Publique | > 360 cm | Disengagement — the counterparty is mentally elsewhere |

**Reading the counterparty's choice.** Where they sit and how far they lean tells you their state. Leaning forward into personal zone during your offer = they like it; pulling back into social zone = they're disengaging or running internal cost calc.

**The positionnement désaxé technique.** When in-person, place yourself at right angles to the counterparty rather than directly opposite. Direct facing reads as competitive (boxing stance); right-angle reads as cooperative (working on a shared problem). Use a corner of the table or a side-by-side seating where possible.

**Video-call proxémie.** On video, watch:

- Are they looking at the camera (engagement) or at the screen (lower engagement) or off-screen (a hidden stakeholder is in the room)?
- Side-glances during answers signal someone they're checking with — usually a procurement minder, an HR observer, or a family member. Surface it: _"Is there someone else who should be on this call to make it productive?"_
- Lean-in vs lean-out is more visible on video than in person — the camera is unforgiving.

### Ruptures de rythme — pace breaks

Sudden shifts in tempo, tone, or energy. Diagnostic value:

- **They suddenly speed up** = often anxiety to close before you push back. Slow your tempo deliberately to break the rhythm.
- **They suddenly slow down** = either tactical (they're trying to control the rhythm) or genuine (they're processing something hard). Match it for one round; if it persists, ask: _"It feels like there's something on your mind — what's coming up?"_
- **They call an unscheduled break** = either tactical (to break a dynamic favourable to you) or defensive (they're saturated). Either way, don't bring concessions to the post-break conversation. Resume from the same anchor.

### Silence diagnosis

Distinguish two kinds of silence:

- _Silence plein_ (loaded silence) — your deliberate weapon. After your offer, after a label, after a calibrated question. 4 seconds minimum. Forces the counterparty to elaborate or commit. The next person to speak loses leverage.
- _Silence vide_ (empty silence) — their tell of lost control. Slack jaw, slowed gestures, reduced blinking. When you see silence vide, hold the silence — they're processing and will likely concede or open up.

## The discovery output

After 1–2 discovery conversations, you should be able to write down:

1. The counterparty's POE per axis (Position / Objectif / Enjeu).
2. For commercial negotiations, the MEDDPICC letters with named people and specific facts. For other domains: stakeholder map + decision criteria + paper-process equivalent.
3. The decision process with named steps and rough dates.
4. The 3 most likely objections in priority order.
5. The political / influence map: champion / advocate, blocker, hidden stakeholders.
6. The counterparty's BATNA estimate (the alternative they're considering, the cost of staying still).

If you can't, you haven't done discovery yet — you've delivered a pitch / a diagnosis / an announcement. Run another conversation.
