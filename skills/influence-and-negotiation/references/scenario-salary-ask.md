# Scenario: Salary ask facing "envelope closed" + counter-offer

**Context.** You're a senior staff engineer at a 600-person growth-stage SaaS company. You've been at $185k base for two years; market for your role is now $215k–$240k. You've taken on tech-lead scope on top of IC work. You raised the ask 3 weeks ago. Your manager just replied: _"I really value your impact. The envelope's closed for this cycle — let's revisit at the next review in 8 months."_ Two days later, a recruiter from a peer company makes a verbal offer at $235k base + $80k sign-on + meaningful equity refresh.

**The trap.** Two parallel traps. (1) Accept the "next review" deferral and let 8 months pass without a structural commitment. (2) Use the external offer as a threat ("match this or I leave"), forcing your manager into reactive matching mode that may resolve the compensation gap but breaks the trust contract — which is half the reason most counter-offer-accepters leave within 12 months anyway.

**The skill's playbook.**

**Phase 3 — Mandascan multi-axis.** Don't reduce this to base. Five axes:

- Base salary
- Equity refresh (size + vesting cliff + acceleration trigger)
- Title / scope (tech-lead recognised, not just absorbed)
- One-off retention bonus (separates from base envelope question)
- Review cadence (cycle-aligned commitment, not vague "next review")

For each, sketch entry / ideal / objective / escalation / rupture (see [references/preparation.md](preparation.md#the-mandascan--5-points-per-axis)).

**Phase 6 — Live response to "envelope closed."** Don't argue the envelope claim and don't fold to it. Use the reality-principle script:

> _"I hear the envelope's closed for the standard cycle, and I appreciate the directness. Help me understand the constraint better. Is it base envelope specifically, total compensation, or department-level? And if base is fixed for this cycle, what would unlock movement — a structural exception (sign-on, equity refresh tied to the tech-lead scope), or a cycle-aligned commitment with a specific number now and a date?"_

This forces the manager to either name the actual constraint (often base specifically — equity, sign-on, and one-off retention are different envelopes) or to escalate.

**On the external offer — the trust frame.** The natural move is to use the offer as leverage. The disciplined move is to share it as **information**, not as a threat:

> _"For context, I'm not actively interviewing, but a recruiter at [adjacent company] made an unprompted offer last week — $235k base, sign-on, equity refresh. I'm sharing because I want you to have full context, not because I'm using it as leverage. I'd rather solve this with you than walk. Help me understand what's possible — and if the answer is genuinely 'nothing structural this cycle,' I'd rather know that now than in 8 months."_

This does three things: (1) puts a real number in the room without a hostage-style threat, (2) explicitly disclaims the leverage move (which procurement-trained managers respect), (3) introduces a firm timeline ("now, not in 8 months") that converts the deferral into a forced choice.

**The escalation move.** If the immediate manager can't or won't move, request the structural conversation: _"Who has the authority on a structural exception? I'd like to make the case once, in front of whoever can approve it, rather than do this in pieces."_ Forces a real conversation rather than a series of soft "I checked, can't do it" replies.

**Likely outcomes.**

- **Best:** company comes back with a sign-on bonus + equity refresh + tech-lead title + commitment to a base raise at the next cycle (specific number, specific date). You stay. Trust intact.
- **Acceptable:** company can't structurally match but offers a real package (e.g., equity refresh + title) that closes 60% of the gap. You decide whether to accept or take the external offer. Either way, decision is informed.
- **Worst case:** company takes the deferral position even after the structured ask. You take the external offer. The conversation taught you something about how this company values retention; that information is useful for your career planning regardless.

**On accepting a counter-offer.** Decision rule: take the counter-offer ONLY if the proposal includes a structural change (new role, new scope, new manager, written cycle-aligned commitment). If it's just a base match, decline — the trust contract has shifted, and statistically half of base-only counter-offer-acceptors leave within 12 months anyway. The trust frame: _"They needed me to threaten to leave before paying me fairly."_

**The patterns illustrated.** Multi-axis Mandascan, not just base. Don't argue the envelope, diagnose it. Share external offers as information, not threat. Force structural exceptions over vague deferrals. Rule for accepting counter-offers: structural change required.
