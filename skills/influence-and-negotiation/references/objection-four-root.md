# The four root commercial objections

Almost all commercial objections reduce to one of four. Knowing which root you're facing tells you which calibrated question to ask first. (Equivalents in salary / NAO domains noted at the end of this section.)

### 1. Price

**Common surface form.** _"It's too expensive."_ / _"We're 30% over budget."_ / _"Your competitor is half the price."_

**Don't immediately defend the price.** Three calibrated questions to surface the real concern:

- _"How was that budget number set?"_ (often reveals the budget is flexible)
- _"What does the alternative offer that we don't?"_ (forces specificity on competitor claims; fake competitors generate platitudes, real ones generate specifics)
- _"If price weren't a factor, would you be choosing us?"_ (separates price from fit; if the answer is no, price was a smokescreen for a different objection)

**Then trade, don't discount.** _"I might be able to find some flexibility on price if we can align on multi-year term and reference rights. Let me explore — what would help most on your side: lower upfront, or lower total?"_

**When the same objection repeats across 3+ calls** — especially price or budget — it is almost never the actual reason. The buyer's stated demand (the **position**) is masking a deeper **enjeu** (career stake, board mandate, internal political risk, fear of being held responsible if it goes wrong). See the POE iceberg (Position / Objectif / Enjeu) in [references/preparation.md](preparation.md). The right next move is not another budget play — it's a calibrated question to your champion targeting their personal stake: _"What's at stake for you personally if this works — or doesn't?"_ The repeated objection won't be solved by re-pitching value; it'll be solved by addressing whatever is making the champion unable to push internally.

### 2. Timing

**Common surface form.** _"Not now."_ / _"Maybe next quarter."_ / _"We're in the middle of a reorg."_

**The trap.** Most timing objections are real. Some are smokescreens for "no need" or "no authority." Diagnose first.

- _"What happens if we miss [their stated date]?"_ — real deadlines have specific consequences; smokescreens don't.
- _"Walk me through what changes between now and Q1 that would make this the right time."_ — real waits have specific upcoming events; smokescreens don't.
- _"What would have to be true for us to start now and ramp slowly?"_ — opens the door to a smaller-footprint deal that bypasses the timing concern.

**If real timing.** Don't fight it. Lock in the future commitment. Use the time to deepen MEDDPICC, multi-thread, and harden the champion.

### 3. Authority

**Common surface form.** _"I'd love to do this but I need to check with [my boss / procurement / security / legal]."_

**The diagnostic.** Distinguish:

- **Real authority gap** — they don't have spend authority. You've been talking to a non-decision-maker. Multi-thread now.
- **Tactical "I need to check"** — the escalation ladder gambit (see procurement awareness below). Concession #1 from your contact, then "my boss won't approve, needs another N%."

Calibrated questions:

- _"Walk me through how a decision like this typically gets made here."_
- _"Who specifically needs to weigh in?"_
- _"What's happened the last time you bought something like this?"_

If they can answer these crisply, the authority gap is real and you have a multi-threading problem. If they can't, the "authority gap" is often tactical and you should name the pattern.

### 4. No need

**Common surface form.** _"We're managing fine with our current setup."_ / _"This isn't a priority right now."_

**The trap.** This is the hardest objection because it's often the truth. You can't sell into "no need" by force; you can only build the cost of inaction until "managing fine" feels like "bleeding slowly."

SPIN's Implication questions:

- _"What happens to your billing accuracy when [the controller / the system] is unavailable?"_
- _"What does the current setup cost you per quarter when it fails?"_
- _"What's the upside if this were 10× better than today?"_

If after this the buyer still says "no need," respect the no. They're right and you're forcing it. Walk away — and watch them quietly come back in 6 months when the cost compounds.

### Cross-domain equivalents of the four root objections

The same four roots appear in other domains under different surface forms. The diagnostic move is the same — calibrated question first, response after.

| Domain | Price equivalent | Timing equivalent | Authority equivalent | No-need equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Salary** | "We're at the top of the band" / "envelope's closed" | "We'll revisit at the next review" | "I need to check with HR / my skip-level" | "You're tracking great where you are" |
| **NAO / social** | "The wage envelope is fixed by the COMEX" | "Let's defer to next round / next year" | "We can't decide that here, it's federation-level" | "Things are stable, no need to renegotiate now" |
| **Internal management** | "I don't have the headcount / promotion budget" | "Let's revisit at the next cycle" | "I'd need to escalate to my N+1 / HR" | "You're doing fine, no change needed" |
| **Recruitment** | "Your compensation expectation is above our band" | "We're not hiring this quarter" | "I need to validate with the hiring committee" | "We're not sure we need this role yet" |

The diagnostic questions translate directly. _"How was that number set?"_ becomes _"How was the band set?"_ in salary, _"How was the wage envelope sized?"_ in NAO. _"What happens if we miss [date]?"_ works unchanged across all domains. _"Walk me through how a decision like this typically gets made here?"_ is the universal authority diagnostic.
